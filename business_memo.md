# Business Memo — What Our Data Tells Us About Customer Churn

**For:** Product, Marketing, and Support teams  
**About:** Why customers are leaving and what we can do about it

---

## The big picture

I looked at data from 2,400 customers to understand who's leaving and why. Turns out, **47% of customers stopped buying** in the 60 days after September 30, 2025. That's almost half.

Here's what I learned.

---

## 1. It's not about who they are — it's about what they do

I checked if things like age, city, or gender affect churn. They don't — at least not much. The difference between age groups is only about 3%. City tiers are even closer.

But **behaviour** tells a very different story:

- Customers who haven't ordered in a while are the most likely to leave (correlation: 0.56)
- Customers who stopped visiting the website are the next biggest risk (correlation: 0.53)
- Customers who order often and spend more tend to stick around

**What this means for the team:** Don't waste time targeting by age group or city. Instead, watch for customers who go quiet — they're the ones about to leave.

---

## 2. How a customer found us matters a lot

| How they found us | % who left |
|-------------------|-----------|
| Organic (found us on their own) | 39.8% |
| Referral (friend recommended) | 42.2% |
| Influencer | 47.6% |
| Marketplace | 49.1% |
| Instagram ads | 49.9% |
| Google Search | 50.4% |

Customers who came through organic search or referrals are much more loyal. Google Search customers churn the most — probably because they were comparing options and aren't committed to our brand.

**What to do:** Invest more in organic content and referral programs. For Google Search customers, create a stronger onboarding experience so they feel connected to the brand from day one.

---

## 3. The loyalty program works — but most people aren't in it

| Loyalty tier | % who left | How many customers |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Platinum | 37.1% | 105 |
| Gold | 40.8% | 319 |
| Silver | 48.8% | 590 |
| Not in program | 48.3% | 1,386 |

Higher tiers clearly have lower churn. The problem? **58% of customers aren't even enrolled.** That's a missed opportunity.

**What to do:** Make it easier to join the loyalty program. Consider auto-enrolling customers after their first purchase. Push upgrades to Gold/Platinum for at-risk customers.

---

## 4. Website activity drops before customers leave

When I compared customers who left vs those who stayed, the ones who left were already showing signs of disengagement:

| What we measured | Customers who stayed | Customers who left |
|-----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Website sessions (30 days) | 6.7 | 4.0 |
| Products viewed | 28.4 | 17.0 |
| Items added to cart | 1.9 | 1.1 |
| Days since last visit | 9.8 | **26.6** |

Churners hadn't visited the website in almost a month on average. Stayers visited less than 10 days ago.

**What to do:** Set up alerts when a customer hasn't visited in 15+ days. Send them a re-engagement email or a personalized offer before they disappear completely.

---

## 5. Complaints don't cause churn

This surprised me. Customers who filed support tickets churn at 46.8%. Those who never complained churn at 47.2%. Almost the same.

Having a complaint doesn't make someone more likely to leave. If anything, reaching out to support shows they still care about the relationship.

**What to do:** Don't panic about support tickets. Focus more on the silent customers who just disappear.
